Abstract

Existing robot manipulation benchmarks treat objects as rigid bodies, ignoring the deformation and topological changes fundamental to food preparation. We introduce CulinaryCut, a physics-aware benchmark for training and evaluating Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models on robotic food-cutting tasks.

Our simulation framework is built on MLS-MPM — a particle-based method that captures material deformation and fracture — coupled with a 7-DoF robot arm in the ManiSkill environment. The benchmark provides over 5,000 expert demonstrations across 7 food categories, paired with rich language annotations (≥5 per trajectory), synchronized end-effector force measurements, and multi-view RGB observations. We define two task families: spatial-based cutting and division-based multi-cut.

We evaluate three state-of-the-art VLA baselines — OpenVLA, Octo, and RDT-1B — and find systematic failures in physical reasoning and multi-step planning that rigid-object benchmarks fail to expose, motivating physics-aware VLA development.

Task Families

Two complementary task families expose distinct capability gaps in current VLA models.

Task Type 01
Spatial Grounding

Spatial-Based Cutting

The robot interprets spatial language — “cut from the left side,” “slice at one-third from the top” — and executes a single downward knife pass at the grounded 3D position. Object pose is randomized to prevent positional overfitting.

Task Type 02
Long-Horizon Planning

Division-Based Multi-Cut

The robot partitions food into N equal pieces through sequential passes. Each cut must account for prior deformation, requiring long-horizon geometric planning. All baselines show the largest performance drop here.

Simulation & Data Collection

  • Physics Engine — MLS-MPM. We use the Moving Least Squares Material Point Method, a particle-based continuum solver that handles large deformations and topology changes (fracture). Material parameters (E, ν, ρ, σy) are set per food type from literature and swept with ±10% Gaussian noise to verify robustness.
  • Robot Environment — ManiSkill. A 7-DoF arm with a knife EEF runs in the GPU-accelerated ManiSkill simulator. Multi-view RGB observations are rendered from randomized camera configurations to avoid viewpoint overfitting.
  • Data Collection. Expert demonstrations are generated via human teleoperation and a motion planner. Each trajectory is annotated with ≥5 paraphrased language instructions and synchronized EEF force logs. Final dataset: 5,000+ base demonstrations — 325K total trajectories.
  • Real-Robot Validation. Force curves from simulation are validated against a physical robot arm executing the same knife motion on real fruit, confirming physics-to-real consistency.
