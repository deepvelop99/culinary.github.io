Overview

Abstract

We present CulinaryCut, a physics-aware benchmark for training and evaluating Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models on robotic food-cutting tasks. Existing manipulation benchmarks treat objects as rigid bodies, ignoring the deformation and topological changes fundamental to food preparation. We bridge this gap with a simulation framework built on MLS-MPM — a particle-based method that captures material deformation and fracture — coupled with a 7-DoF robot arm in the ManiSkill environment. Our benchmark provides over 5,000 expert demonstrations across 7 food categories, paired with rich language annotations (≥5 per trajectory), synchronized end-effector force measurements, and multi-view RGB observations. Two task families expose distinct failure modes: spatial-based cutting (single-pass positional grounding) and division-based multi-cut (long-horizon sequential planning with cumulative deformation). We evaluate three state-of-the-art VLA baselines — OpenVLA, Octo, and RDT-1B — and reveal systematic failures in physical reasoning and multi-step planning that rigid-object benchmarks fail to surface, motivating the development of physics-aware VLA architectures.

Benchmark Design

Task Families

Two complementary tasks expose distinct capability gaps in vision-language-action models.

TYPE 01

Spatial-Based Cutting

The robot interprets spatial language — "cut from the left side," "slice at one-third from the top" — and executes a single downward knife motion at the grounded position. Tests VLA spatial language comprehension in a 3-D workspace with randomized object poses.

TYPE 02

Division-Based Multi-Cut

The robot partitions food into N equal pieces through sequential passes. Each cut must account for material deformation from prior cuts, requiring long-horizon geometric planning. The largest performance gap between VLA baselines and optimal occurs here.
