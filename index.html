Abstract

Existing robot manipulation benchmarks treat objects as rigid bodies, ignoring the deformation and topological changes fundamental to food preparation. We introduce CulinaryCut, a physics-aware benchmark for training and evaluating Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models on robotic food-cutting tasks.

Our simulation framework is built on MLS-MPM — a particle-based method that captures material deformation and fracture — coupled with a 7-DoF robot arm in the ManiSkill environment. The benchmark provides over 5,000 expert demonstrations across 7 food categories, paired with rich language annotations (≥5 per trajectory), synchronized end-effector force measurements, and multi-view RGB observations. We define two task families: spatial-based cutting, which requires grounding of positional language in 3D, and division-based multi-cut, which demands long-horizon sequential planning under cumulative material deformation.

We evaluate three state-of-the-art VLA baselines — OpenVLA, Octo, and RDT-1B — and find systematic failures in physical reasoning and multi-step planning that rigid-object benchmarks fail to expose. Our results reveal the largest performance gap on multi-cut tasks, motivating the development of physics-aware VLA architectures.

Food Categories

CulinaryCut spans three orders of magnitude in Young's modulus (7 kPa → 1 GPa) with material properties from food science literature. Each fruit is procedurally meshed and assigned physics-grounded parameters (Young's modulus E, Poisson's ratio ν, density ρ, yield stress σy).

Task Families

CulinaryCut defines two complementary task families that expose distinct capability gaps in current VLA models.

Task Type 01

Spatial-Based Cutting

The robot interprets spatial language instructions — "cut from the left side," "slice at one-third from the top" — and executes a single downward knife motion at the grounded 3D position. Object pose is randomized across episodes to prevent positional overfitting.

Task Type 02

Division-Based Multi-Cut

The robot partitions food into N equal pieces through sequential cutting passes. Each pass must account for material deformation from prior cuts, requiring long-horizon geometric planning. All VLA baselines exhibit the largest performance drop on this task family.

Simulation & Data Collection

Physics Engine — MLS-MPM. We use the Moving Least Squares Material Point Method (MLS-MPM), a particle-based continuum solver that naturally handles large deformations and topology changes (fracture). Material parameters are assigned per food type from literature and swept across ±10% Gaussian noise to establish robustness to parameter uncertainty.

Robot Environment — ManiSkill. A 7-DoF robot arm with a knife end-effector operates inside the ManiSkill simulator. The simulation runs on GPU, enabling high-throughput parallel data collection. Multi-view RGB observations are rendered from randomized camera configurations.

Data Collection. Expert demonstrations are generated via human teleoperation (spatial tasks) and a motion planner (multi-cut tasks). Each trajectory is annotated with ≥5 paraphrased natural language instructions and synchronized end-effector force logs. The dataset contains 5,000+ base demonstrations and 325K total trajectories after augmentation.

Baseline Comparison

We fine-tune three state-of-the-art VLA models on CulinaryCut demonstrations and evaluate on held-out episodes. The largest performance gap appears on division-based multi-cut tasks. Full quantitative results and ablation studies are in the paper.
